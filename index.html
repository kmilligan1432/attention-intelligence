The Problem

You're not falling apart.
You're carrying too much.

You're in everything. The decisions. The logistics. The emotional weight that no one else sees. The mental list that never ends.

Your brain is tracking it all: the people who need you, the responsibilities you can't hand off, the things you're terrified of dropping.

Even when you stop, you're not really stopped. Your nervous system never fully comes down. You lie awake running tomorrow's list. You feel guilty when you rest. You've forgotten what it feels like to be fully present with the people you love.

The real risk isn't falling behind. It's a life not fully lived. Fractured relationships. Lost meaning. The feeling of being hollow inside while everything around you looks like it's working. That's the cost of never seeing what truly deserves your attention.
The Thesis

This is not an
efficiency problem.

Everyone is talking about using AI to do more, move faster, automate everything. I believe they're solving the wrong problem. The crisis isn't productivity. It's attention.

Most people have never mapped the full web of meaning in their life. The roles you play, the family dynamics and generational patterns that shaped how you think, love, and lead. In the chaos of constant doing, that entire web becomes invisible. You can't see it. So you can't live from it. And when you can't live from it, nothing feels meaningful. Even when you're succeeding by every external measure.

There has never been another you, and there never will be again. The odds of you existing at all are roughly 1 in 102,685,000. As Bill Bryson wrote, for 3.8 billion years, every single one of your ancestors survived long enough, found each other, and created the exact chain of events that led to you. That configuration has never existed before. You are not replaceable. The world does not get another version of what you were meant to bring to it.

This is not about adding pressure. Pressure is what got you here. This is about seeing clearly enough to become calm. When you can finally see the full picture of what you're carrying, the stress quiets. Not because anything external changed, but because your mind is no longer spinning in the dark. You have a thinking partner holding the complexity with you. And from that calm, you become more present. More whole. More you.

That ripples. Your children get to watch you live from who you really are, so they learn how to live from who they really are. Your relationships deepen. The people who need what only you can give them finally receive it. Not a warning. An invitation.

There's an old idea that how deeply you love someone is measured by the quality of attention you give them. I believe that. And I believe we're living in a moment where that attention is under siege. Not by distraction, but by a life that was never designed around what actually matters to you.

I call this Attention Intelligence. The ability to see the full web of your life clearly, understand the family systems and archetypes that shaped you, and direct your focus toward what gives it meaning. AI, used correctly, doesn't replace your judgment. It becomes a thinking partner that helps you recover it. Mapping what you could never see alone, and then paying attention to it.

This is the work I'm building. Through consulting, speaking, the More Human newsletter, and Oliza.ai - a platform designed to make AI a thinking partner, not a replacement for thinking.

About

Why this work
matters to me.

For most of my life, I believed I could handle anything. And for a long time, I did.

In my late twenties, my marriage ended. I had to start over with nothing. And from that rebuilding came everything: Spark Evolution, my children, and eventually an Amazon brand I built and sold for millions in under two years, outselling Hallmark and American Greetings in their own category.

I grew PureFlix into HubSpot's largest client contact database at 7 million contacts. I reengineered hospital departments at 17. In 2008, while competitors were closing their doors, my agency grew. Earning the Make Mine a Million Dollar Business Award through American Express during the financial crisis.

But the real systems challenge came in 2019, when a six-year siege of family crisis began. My father's hospice. My mother's broken leg. A pandemic. My husband's brother diagnosed with terminal cancer. And then, in January 2024, our four-year-old daughter Eliza was diagnosed with leukemia.

I used AI to research treatment protocols, organize the overwhelming logistics of care, and stay present for our son Ollie, who held his sister's hand through all of it. I am not a medical professional. I am a mother who refused to be helpless.

The AI "thinking partners" I built to survive were exactly what every person carrying too much needs to find themselves again. I don't just understand systems. I understand the weight of the human carrying them.

Our AI platform is called Oliza, a blend of Ollie and Eliza's names. Eliza rings the bell in May 2026. Every company I've built, every stage I walk onto, every talk I give. It is all for them. That is not a tagline. That is the whole story.

Before It's Too Late

At the end of life, no one regrets
not sending that email.

  • "I wish I'd had the courage to live a life true to myself."
  • "I wish I hadn't worked so hard."
  • "I wish I'd had the courage to express my feelings."
  • "I wish I had stayed in touch with my friends."
  • "I wish I had let myself be happier."
From Bronnie Ware's research with patients in palliative care. The Top Five Regrets of the Dying

Every single one of these is an attention problem. Not a time problem. Not a productivity problem. These are the words of people who had time. But never mapped what made their life meaningful. They never saw the web. Their unique gifts, their irreplaceable roles, the things only they could bring to the world.

They were busy. They were responsible. They were needed. They were running like they were running out of time. But they had time. What they didn't have was their attention in the right places. And they missed the beauty of their own lives because of it.

I know because I was living that way too. Before my daughter was diagnosed with leukemia, I was so deep in the doing that I was missing it. The life that was right in front of me. Her diagnosis was my wake-up call.

You don't have to wait for illness. You don't have to wait for loss. You don't have to wait for the moment that forces you to see what was always there.

You can start paying attention to the right things now.

That's what AI is actually capable of. Not replacing you. Not making you faster. But giving you back the clarity to see what matters. While you still have time to live it.

Live a life you'll never regret.
Start by giving your attention to what deserves it.
